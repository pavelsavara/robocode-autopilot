# Intuition Phase 6 — Wave Stacking Survey

*2026-05-03 — Notebook 12 executed against the 50-bot / 8-battle rumble
sample (400 perspectives, 737k wave events, 14k score rows). First
empirical scan of multi-bullet firing patterns in the competitive pool.*

## Data

| Dataset | Rows | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| waves.csv | 737,381 | One row per detected opponent fire |
| scores.csv | 14,000 | One row per round per perspective |
| Consecutive pairs | 723,419 | Adjacent fires within same round |
| Consecutive triplets | 709,457 | Three adjacent fires within same round |

## Findings

### 1. Every competitive bot uses variable fire power

All 50 bots fire across the full [0.1, 3.0] power range. Std dev ranges
from 0.50 to 0.92. The most variable:

| Bot | Power mean | Power std |
|---|---|---|
| Roborio 1.2.4 | 1.02 | 0.92 |
| DrussGT 3.1.7 | 1.16 | 0.88 |
| Firestarter 2.0f | 1.07 | 0.86 |
| BeepBoop 2.0 | 0.81 | 0.85 |
| Knight 0.6.28 | 0.93 | 0.85 |

No bot fires constant power — adaptive power selection is universal.
This is prerequisite for wave stacking, but none appear to do it
intentionally.

### 2. Slow→fast firing patterns are rare and incidental

Only 1–4% of consecutive fire pairs show a significant speed increase
(>2 px/tick between shots). Top: Engineer (4.2%), CassiusClay (4.0%),
Cardigan (3.2%), DrussGT (3.1%).

The average tick gap between consecutive fires is consistently ~14 ticks
(natural gun cooldown from power ~1.0), not tuned for convergence at
specific distances. These patterns arise from adaptive power selection,
not deliberate wave stacking.

### 3. Near-simultaneous wave arrivals occur at 500–700 px

7,742 pairs (1.1% of all consecutive fires) produce arrivals within
≤5 ticks of each other. Distance statistics:

| Stat | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean | 577 px |
| Median | 583 px |
| 25th percentile | 492 px |
| 75th percentile | 688 px |

This is higher than the theoretical 400 px sweet spot for our
deliberate 3.0→0.1 power pairing because most bots fire moderate
power deltas (e.g. 2.0→1.0), which converge at longer range.

Top producers: Domogled (1,442), BeepBoop (1,287), Neuromancer (1,056),
Firestarter (768), Diamond (636). These are bots with the most variable
power (high std) and high fire rates.

### 4. Multi-wave is the norm, not the exception

**96.3% of fire events** happen while 2+ opponent waves are already in
flight. This is structural: bullet flight time (15–40 ticks) exceeds
gun cooldown (~14 ticks), so the previous bullet hasn't arrived when
the next one fires. Max concurrent waves observed: **19** (Neuromancer).

**Implication**: Multi-wave defense is mandatory, not a luxury feature.
The `IWaveDanger` multi-wave interface in the robot architecture is
essential even against non-stacking opponents.

### 5. Three-shot 2+1 patterns exist at 0.8% frequency

| Pattern | Count | % of triplets |
|---|---|---|
| Decreasing power (heavy→medium→light) | 87,224 | 12.3% |
| 2+1 convergence (first two ≤3t, all ≤10t) | 5,724 | 0.8% |
| Full triple convergence (all ≤5t) | 5,608 | 0.8% |

The 2+1 and full-triple counts are nearly identical (5,724 vs 5,608),
meaning almost every case where the first two converge also has the
third close behind. Median distance: **616 px**.

Same top producers as 2-bullet convergence: Domogled (1,341), BeepBoop
(1,180), Neuromancer (961). These are incidental — the bots' adaptive
power selection happens to create convergence at longer ranges.

### 6. Wave convergence does NOT improve opponent hit rate

**This is the key negative finding.** Rounds where the opponent produced
near-simultaneous wave arrivals had *lower* hit rates, not higher:

| Category | Rounds | Mean hit rate | Median |
|---|---|---|---|
| No convergence | 12,512 | **0.1210** | 0.1163 |
| Has 2-bullet pairs | 1,425 | 0.1127 | 0.1111 |
| Has 2+1 triplets | 252 | **0.0787** | 0.0859 |

The within-bot paired comparison (controlling for observer identity)
confirms: mean delta = **−0.0031** (convergence *hurts* the attacker).
Wilcoxon signed-rank test: **p = 0.60, not significant.** Only 23 of 50
bots show positive delta.

### Why convergence doesn't help (yet)

Three explanations:

1. **These are incidental convergences, not aimed ones.** When a bot's
   adaptive power selection happens to produce near-simultaneous arrivals,
   the two bullets were aimed at the same GF profile — they reinforce
   each other rather than creating complementary danger zones. Deliberate
   wave stacking would aim bullet 2 at a *different* GF than bullet 1,
   exploiting the knowledge that the opponent dodged bullet 1.

2. **Convergence correlates with distance.** Median convergence distance
   is 577 px — long range where *all* hit rates are lower because the
   opponent has more time to dodge. The convergence benefit (if any)
   doesn't overcome the distance penalty.

3. **The 2+1 triplet paradox.** Triplets have the lowest hit rate (0.079)
   because they occur almost exclusively in long-range, low-power-mean
   fights — exactly the conditions where all bots miss more.

### What deliberate wave stacking would look like

To actually exploit wave stacking:

- Fire bullet 1 at **power 3.0** at the peak of the opponent's current GF
  profile.
- Fire bullet 2 at **power 0.1** aimed at the opponent's *predicted dodge
  position* — i.e., aim where they'll be after dodging bullet 1.
- Target distance **350–450 px** where both bullets arrive within 1–3
  ticks, not 500–700 px where incidental convergences occur.

This requires:
1. The opponent's GF profile (from `VcsWaveDanger` / MLP targeting)
2. A model of where the opponent will dodge to (from the movement
   predictor — GBM-window at N=5 is R²=0.735)
3. Deliberate power scheduling (`IFirePlan`)

No bot in the rumble pool does this. It would be a genuinely novel tactic.

### 7. Damage-weighted surfing makes wave stacking ineffective against top bots

The damage asymmetry between heavy and light bullets is extreme:

| Bullet | Power | Damage | Energy return | Net swing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy | 3.0 | 16.0 | +9.0 | 25.0 |
| Medium | 1.0 | 4.0 | +3.0 | 7.0 |
| Light | 0.1 | 0.4 | +0.3 | 0.7 |

In a 3.0 + 0.1 pair, the heavy bullet does 40× more damage. A surfer
that weights wave danger by `damage(power)` allocates 97.6% of its
dodge effort to the heavy wave and effectively ignores the light one.
Absorbing the 0.1 bullet costs 0.4 energy — less than one tick of gun
heat.

DrussGT explicitly scales danger by bullet damage. Diamond and other
top bots do the same. Wave stacking cannot confuse these surfers because
the light bullet's GF peak pulls the dodge position by only ~2.4% of
the total weight.

**Vulnerable bots**: Mid-tier surfers (BasicSurfer derivatives) that
weight all waves equally. For them, the light bullet's GF peak on the
opposite side from the heavy bullet's peak creates a genuine compromise
problem — the surfer tries to minimize combined danger and ends up in
a suboptimal position for both waves.

**Implication for offense**: Wave stacking is a niche anti-mid-tier
tactic, not a universal competitive edge. Against the top 10 bots in
our rumble pool, damage-weighted surfing neutralizes it.

**Implication for defense**: Our `IWaveDanger` multi-wave implementation
must weight by `wave.damage()`. This single design choice makes us
immune to wave-stacking attacks and ensures we always prioritize
survival against the most lethal incoming bullet.

## Conclusions

1. **Multi-wave defense is mandatory** — 96% of combat involves 2+
   concurrent waves. This validates the `IWaveDanger` multi-wave
   interface design in the robot architecture.

2. **Incidental wave convergence doesn't help the attacker** — p=0.60,
   no significant effect. The convergences in the data are unintentional
   and uncoordinated.

3. **Deliberate wave stacking remains an untested competitive edge** —
   no bot in the pool does it intentionally. Our `WaveStackPlan` /
   `TripleStackPlan` would be novel. The theoretical benefit (forcing
   the opponent into a constrained multi-wave dodge) is sound, but the
   empirical benefit is unknown and cannot be measured from existing data.
   It requires building and testing.

4. **Priority adjustment**: Wave stacking is a niche anti-mid-tier
   offense tactic — damage-weighted surfers (DrussGT, Diamond) are
   immune. Multi-wave defense with damage weighting is the high-priority
   item: it addresses the proven 96% multi-wave combat frequency AND
   makes us immune to wave-stacking attacks.

5. **Damage-weighted wave scoring is the single most important design
   choice** for the `IWaveDanger` interface. Weight each wave's danger
   contribution by `damage(power)`. This collapses the wave-stacking
   threat to ~2.4% of the scoring weight for cheap bullets.

## Addendum: Multi-Wave GF Distribution Analysis (nb13)

*Notebook 13 / `_run_multiwave.py` — 30 bots × 2 battles, 101k waves,
1.88M ticks.*

### Question

Does the opponent's GF position at wave-break change when they're under
multi-wave pressure? If yes, this is a targetable feature for the MLP.

### Results

**Yes — statistically significant but modest.**

| Wave pressure | n | GF mean | GF std | |GF mean| |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| none (0 other waves) | 3,553 | −0.082 | **0.309** | 0.191 |
| moderate (1 other wave) | 35,768 | −0.082 | 0.307 | 0.191 |
| high (2+ other waves) | 58,614 | −0.075 | **0.286** | 0.175 |

- **KS none vs moderate**: 0.020, p=0.14 (NOT significant)
- **KS none vs high**: 0.050, p=1.3×10⁻⁷ (significant)

Under high wave pressure, GF std drops from 0.309 to 0.286 (−7%) and
|mean GF| drops from 0.191 to 0.175 (−8%). Opponents dodge less far from
center when under pressure — their reachable set is more constrained.

### Inter-wave gap matters more

| Gap category | n | GF std | |GF mean| |
|---|---|---|---|
| tight (≤5 ticks) | 1,037 | **0.258** | **0.138** |
| moderate (6–15 ticks) | 65,614 | 0.300 | 0.185 |
| loose (>15 ticks) | 27,731 | 0.282 | 0.172 |

- **KS tight vs loose**: 0.145, p=9.2×10⁻¹⁹ (highly significant)

When waves arrive within 5 ticks of each other, GF std drops to 0.258
(−17% vs no-pressure) and |mean GF| drops to 0.138 (−28%). This is the
strongest signal: tight inter-wave gaps genuinely constrain opponent
movement. The opponent stays closer to GF=0 (head-on position) because
there's no safe dodge direction for both waves.

### Per-bot sensitivity

18 of 30 bots narrow their GF spread under pressure; 12 widen. Most
sensitive (highest KS): Knight (0.151), SilverSurfer (0.149),
Midboss (0.131), Chalk (0.126), PowerHouse (0.124).

PowerHouse is the only bot that significantly widens under pressure
(std 0.105 → 0.147, delta +0.042) — it may be using a deliberate
flattening strategy that activates under multi-wave conditions.

### ML feature importance

Adding wave-pressure features to a GF-bin RF classifier shows zero
accuracy improvement (0.6088 → 0.6088). However, the feature importance
tells a different story:

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---|
| fire_distance | 0.290 |
| bullet_speed | 0.202 |
| **total_wave_damage** | **0.182** |
| **nearest_wave_gap** | **0.158** |
| bullet_power | 0.134 |
| n_waves_in_flight | 0.033 |

`total_wave_damage` and `nearest_wave_gap` carry **34% combined
importance** — they're informative but don't improve accuracy because
the RF already saturates on this small feature set. The MLP with its
richer 18-feature input and 61-bin output is more likely to benefit.

### Conclusions for MLP targeting

1. **Add 3 features**: `n_waves_in_flight`, `nearest_wave_gap`,
   `total_wave_damage` to the MLP input (15 → 18 dimensions, +384
   parameters, negligible cost).

2. **The offline edge is real**: The GF-narrowing effect under tight
   wave gaps (KS=0.145, p=10⁻¹⁹) is learnable from 100k+ waves but
   invisible within a single battle's ~200 waves. No online VCS can
   segment by wave pressure without drowning in sparse bins.

3. **Priority**: Medium. The effect is modest (−7% std under general
   pressure, −17% under tight gaps) but free to capture — it's just
   3 extra input features computed from our own wave list.
