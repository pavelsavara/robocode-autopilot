# Intuition Phase 3 — Wave Analysis & Schema Evolution

*2026-05-02 — Notebook 08, ~737k wave events from 399 battles, 50 bot families*

This phase covered three things: a `win_rate` bug fix in the pipeline, the introduction of split bot-identity hashes plus per-battle constants in `scores.csv`, and a new wave-analysis notebook that profiles bot personalities from their fire events alone.

## Schema Evolution

### `win_rate` bug fix
`Whiteboard.incrementRoundsWon/Lost()` was only called from `Autopilot.onWin/onDeath` in the live robot — the offline pipeline never credited round winners, so `scores.csv:win_rate` was 0.000 for every row. Fixed in `Player.finalizeRound()`, called by `Main` before each `writeScoreRow`. Logic mirrors Robocode itself: alive beats dead; both alive at timeout → higher energy wins; equal energy or simultaneous KO → tie.

### Three identity hashes (replacing single `opponent_name_hash`)
Robocode's `getName()` returns `"<class> <version>"` (e.g. `DrussGT 3.1.7`). The old single hash made `DrussGT 3.1.7` and `DrussGT 3.1.8` look like unrelated bots, defeating cross-battle ML segmentation.

| Column | Hash of | ML role |
|---|---|---|
| `opponent_name_hash` | full string | tightest segmentation — same exact build |
| `opponent_bot_id_hash` | part before first space | **bot family** — survives version bumps. Primary fingerprint. |
| `opponent_version_hash` | part after first space | distinguishes patches |

`Whiteboard.setOpponentScan()` splits on the first ASCII space; defensive fallback if no space → botId = full name, version = `""`. `IdentityFeatures` is now null-safe and the `fnv1a32()` helper is `public static`.

### Per-battle constants moved to `scores.csv`
A value like `battlefield_width` is constant for an entire battle. Repeating it 3,000× per battle in `ticks.csv` wastes ~24 KB/battle for zero information gain. Solution: route slow-moving dimensions to `scores.csv` (≤35 rows/battle, ~280 bytes/battle). Notebooks deduplicate via `scores.drop_duplicates('battle_id')` or join into ticks via the new `attach_battle_constants()` helper.

`scores.csv` now carries 7 new columns:
- `opponent_name_hash`, `opponent_bot_id_hash`, `opponent_version_hash`
- `battlefield_width`, `battlefield_height`, `gun_cooling_rate`, `num_rounds_total`

`opponent_name_hash` was **removed** from `ticks.csv` (was wasted bytes there).

### Convention codified in `.github/copilot-instructions.md`
New section "CSV layout — pick the right granularity" with the rule: when adding a feature, ask *does it change every tick?* If not, route it to `scores.csv`. Per-fire-event snapshots → `waves.csv`. Per-tick state-of-the-world → `ticks.csv`.

## Notebook 08 — Wave Analysis

**Dataset:** 737,381 wave events (one row per detected opponent fire) across 399 battles, 50 bot families, 247 MB pandas memory.

**Why waves matter:** Every fire is a real decision the opponent made. Their fire-power distribution and preferred fire-distance are stable, low-variance bot fingerprints — much cleaner signals than noisy per-tick movement.

### Pipeline physics sanity check (Section 2)

| Identity | Max residual | 99th percentile |
|---|---:|---:|
| `bullet_speed = 20 - 3·power` | 0.015 | 0.015 |
| `MEA = asin(8 / bullet_speed)` | 5×10⁻⁵ | 2×10⁻⁵ |
| `flight_time = fire_distance / bullet_speed` | 0.007 | 0.005 |

All residuals are at CSV float-printing precision. Pipeline numbers match the Robocode rulebook exactly — any pattern found below is real bot behavior, not measurement noise.

### Power–distance trade-off (Section 5)

**Pooled correlation: r = -0.337.** Closer = more power, as the textbook prescribes.

**Most adaptive bots** (strongest negative power-distance correlation):

| Family | n | Correlation | Mean power | Mean dist |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Garm | 9,881 | **-0.79** | 1.59 | 523 |
| Domogled | 17,455 | -0.48 | 0.68 | 583 |
| RougeDC | 15,077 | -0.43 | 1.19 | 401 |

| Neuromancer | 41,511 | -0.37 | 0.68 | 547 |

**Fixed-power bots** (correlation ≈ 0): Firebird, Pris, Wintermute. They fire the same power regardless of range.

Knowing this lets us pre-classify an opponent's targeting style after a single short observation window: a steep negative correlation is a bullet-power-aware bot, predictable in its energy economy.

### Opportunism (Section 6)

`wave_lateral_velocity_at_fire` records **our** sideways speed at the instant they fired. Low values mean they wait for slow targets.

**Most opportunistic** (mean |lat-vel-at-fire| ≤ 4.7, vs theoretical max of 8):

| Family | Mean | Median | n |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| PowerHouse | 3.80 | 3.79 | 15,668 |
| Phoenix | 4.07 | 4.02 | 18,482 |
| Holden | 4.21 | 4.88 | 13,234 |
| Chalk | 4.44 | 5.03 | 14,421 |
| deBroglie | 4.60 | 5.04 | 4,599 |

These bots wait for our tangential speed to drop before pulling the trigger — they have implicit "easy target" detection. Beating them means **never** decelerating laterally during gun-cooling windows.

## Implications for ML

1. **Use `opponent_bot_id_hash` as the primary segmentation key**, not `opponent_name_hash`. It survives version bumps so the model can pool data across `DrussGT 3.1.7` and `DrussGT 3.1.8`.
2. **Wave fingerprints are cheap labels.** Six stats × ~50 fires gives a workable classifier into "aggressive / conservative / adaptive / opportunistic" archetypes within the first round of a match.
3. **Power-distance correlation is a single-number predictor of targeting style.** Worth surfacing as an explicit feature (computed per-battle from cumulative waves) rather than letting the model rediscover it.
4. **Lateral-velocity-at-fire is exploitable both ways.** Against opportunistic firers (PowerHouse, Phoenix), maintain non-zero |lat-vel| during their gun-cooling windows. We can compute it ourselves from `OUR_LATERAL_VELOCITY` on each detected fire and feed back as a meta-signal.
5. **The pipeline is faithful.** Physics residuals are at print precision — no need for further scaffolding tests on bullet kinematics.

## Leakage audit — inputs vs outcomes

A walk through the pipeline confirmed a clean input/outcome separation, and the question "are waves recorded at fire-time or hit-time?" has a single-line answer: **fire-detection time, no hit/pass column exists**. Worth pinning down so future ML work can rely on it.

### Wave row timing
[`EnergyFeatures.process()`](../../core/src/main/java/cz/zamboch/autopilot/core/features/EnergyFeatures.java) sets `OPPONENT_FIRED = 1` on the first scan tick where `prevEnergy − currEnergy ∈ [0.1, 3.0]` and the drop is not explained by our hit or a wall collision. [`WaveTrackingOfflineFeatures`](../../pipeline/src/main/java/cz/zamboch/autopilot/pipeline/features/WaveTrackingOfflineFeatures.java) snapshots `bullet_power`, derived physics (`speed`, `MEA`, `flight_time`), and same-tick `DISTANCE` and `OUR_LATERAL_VELOCITY`. [`Main`](../../pipeline/src/main/java/cz/zamboch/autopilot/pipeline/Main.java) writes one row to `waves.csv` exclusively when `OPPONENT_FIRED == 1.0`.

**Caveat — one-tick fire-detection lag.** The energy drop is visible only on the *next* scan after the bullet leaves the gun. So `wave_lateral_velocity_at_fire` is really "lateral velocity ~1 tick after the trigger pull". Stable across all bots (so it doesn't bias inter-bot comparisons), and at 8 px/tick max the position drift is ≤8 px against fire distances of hundreds of px. Worth a Javadoc note on `WaveTrackingOfflineFeatures` so future callers don't get confused.

### File-by-file leakage classification

| File | Row granularity | Contents | Leakage risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ticks.csv` | per tick | state-of-world (positions, velocities, headings, distances, gun heat, `opponent_fired` flag) | None for movement prediction. `opponent_energy` is a *soft* outcome — fine as a feature, but late-round it correlates almost perfectly with `win_rate`. |
| `waves.csv` | per fire event | fire-time snapshot only. **No hit/pass outcome.** | None today. Future hit-outcome columns (`wave_was_hit`, `realised_dodge_fraction`) must stay in waves.csv only — never propagated back into ticks.csv. |
| `scores.csv` | per round | outcomes (`damage_dealt`, `damage_received`, `our_hit_rate`, `opponent_hit_rate`, `win_rate`) **+** per-battle constants | High if joined naively. Mitigated by `_loader.py::BATTLE_CONSTANT_COLS` whitelist. |

### Architectural safeguards

- **`BATTLE_CONSTANT_COLS` whitelist** in `intuition/_loader.py` enumerates only the seven non-outcome columns from `scores.csv` that `attach_battle_constants()` will merge into ticks/waves. The outcome columns (`damage_*`, `*_hit_rate`, `win_rate`) are deliberately not in that list, so the standard merge helper cannot leak them.
- **Cross-perspective isolation.** Each battle writes `<battle_id>/RobotA/` and `<battle_id>/RobotB/` from independent `Whiteboard`s — no cross-reads. Notebook 08 only loads one perspective at a time and labels via the sibling-directory name (no data read from the sibling). Any future "does our movement predict our hit rate" model must pick one side and stick with it; mixing the two is perfect leakage of the answer.
- **Convention codified.** A new "Don't leak outcomes into learning features" subsection in `.github/copilot-instructions.md` documents these rules so future feature additions and notebook merges respect them.

### Concrete recommendations

1. **Document the one-tick fire-detection lag** in `WaveTrackingOfflineFeatures` Javadoc so future ML callers know `wave_lateral_velocity_at_fire` is `t+1`, not `t`. One-line fix.
2. **Future hit/pass columns route via `FileType.WAVES` only.** Already covered by the realised-dodge metric below; flagging here so the rule is explicit.
3. **Treat `opponent_energy` as semi-outcome.** For per-tick movement prediction (notebook 05) it's fine. For round-outcome prediction (notebook 07), exclude or cap to early-round ticks — late in a round it's virtually `1 − win_rate`.

## Future Work

- **Realised-dodge metric.** Join each wave with the next ~`wave_flight_time` ticks of our movement (from ticks.csv, on the same `battle_id, robot_name`) to compute the fraction of MEA we actually used. This quantifies a specific opponent's targeting accuracy *against our movement*, not just their abstract style.
- **Wave-fingerprint clustering at K=4.** The current PCA scatter is suggestive; explicit KMeans (K=4) plus annotation against known Robocode archetypes (head-on aimers, GF guns, pattern-matchers, anti-surfers) would validate the natural axes.
- **Add `our_realised_lat_vel_during_flight` to the waves schema.** Captures our actual dodge speed *during* the bullet's flight, not just at fire instant — directly comparable to MEA budget. One extra column per wave, ~50–200 rows/battle, negligible cost.
- **Notebook 9 — adaptation across rounds.** Re-run the wave fingerprint per round (instead of pooled) to detect bots whose targeting evolves mid-match. Pairs naturally with Phase 2's KS-test adaptation analysis on movement.
