schema. All five executed cleanly on the 1944-battle / 50-bot rumble
dataset; results below are the first ML numbers from this codebase that

## What the new runs revealed

### 1. Persistence-baseline R² is *negative* for lateral-velocity prediction

Notebook 05's N-step lateral-velocity model finally gives an honest baseline.
"Predict the same value as the current tick" — the textbook persistence
baseline — has R² between **−0.89 and −1.10** at horizons N=5/10/20:

| N | Persistence MAE | Persistence R² | RF MAE | RF R² | Improvement |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 5  | 6.08 px | **−0.89** | 4.66 px | 0.07 | 23 % |
| 10 | 6.53 px | **−1.10** | 4.72 px | 0.04 | 28 % |
| 20 | 6.37 px | **−1.03** | 4.76 px | 0.02 | 25 % |

Negative R² means the persistence prediction is worse than just predicting
the mean. Lateral velocity at tick *t+5* is essentially uncorrelated with
lateral velocity at tick *t* — opponents have already changed direction. The
RF is barely above the mean (R² ≤ 0.07), which lines up with the Phase 4
finding that **per-tick movement autocorrelation is ≤ 0.03 at every lag for
every bot**. There is genuine signal (25 % MAE reduction) but it comes from
state context, not from autoregression. Anyone building a movement model
should expect this — fitting a future tick from the current tick alone is
not just hard, it's anti-informative.

### 2. The K=5 movement-archetype clustering reproduces the famous trio

Notebook 05's silhouette curve picks K=5 (score 0.208). The interesting
cluster is the smallest:

> **Cluster 3 (3 bots): BeepBoop 2.0, DrussGT 3.1.7, Seraphim 2.3.1.**

These are the three bots whose movement profiles separate from everyone
else — and BeepBoop / DrussGT are the literal top-2 in `scores.csv`'s 50-bot
win-rate table (next section). The clustering is unsupervised on six raw
movement summaries (lat-vel std, abs vel mean, heading delta, dir-change
time, decel fraction, wall distance) yet picks them out. That's a real
finding: top-tier movement *looks* different in the most basic statistics,
not just in the head-to-head outcome.

The other clusters are more diffuse (24 / 12 / 7 / 3 bots).

### 4. Round-outcome classification: the first honest 70 %

Notebook 07's per-round model (`net_damage > 0` from per-round
state-of-world aggregates) is the headline honest result of this phase:

```
Round Outcome Prediction (1,036 test rounds):
  Majority baseline: 0.506
  Train accuracy:    0.847
  Test accuracy:     0.706
```

20 percentage points of lift over the majority baseline, with **no leakage**
— the inputs are aggregates of `ticks.csv` columns (distance, lat-vel std,
energy ratio, heading delta std, wall distance, fire counts) and the label
is the per-round `(win_rate ≥ 0.5)` indicator. Top features:

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---:|
| `energy_ratio_mean` | 0.36 |
| `wall_dist_mean` | 0.10 |
| `our_lat_vel_std` | 0.09 |
| `distance_mean` | 0.08 |
| `opp_heading_delta_std` | 0.08 |

`energy_ratio_mean` carrying 36 % is intuitive: rounds you win are rounds
where your average energy lead was higher. But notice it's not 100 % — there
is genuine residual signal in distance / wall hugging / our own lat-vel
variability after controlling for energy.

This is the right shape of an honest Robocode model: ~70 % accuracy, modest
lift, multi-feature contribution. Anything claiming 1.000 should now be
audited against this baseline.

### 5. Fire-power prediction: leakage moved one layer down (now fixed)

Notebook 07's fire-power task was supposed to land in the 0.5–0.8 R² range
after dropping `opponent_bullet_speed` and `mea_for_opponent_bullet`. It
didn't:

```
Fire Power Prediction (11,059 test samples) — leaky run #1
  Random Forest: MAE=0.000, R²=1.000
  Top feature:  opponent_inferred_gun_heat (0.894)
```

The leakage path is more subtle than a same-tick algebraic identity.
`opponent_inferred_gun_heat` is the pipeline's reconstruction of the
opponent's gun-cooldown counter, which decrements every tick from
`1 + power/5` after each detected fire. Our pipeline observes the fire
**one tick after the energy drop**, so at the fire-detection row the
counter has just jumped to `1 + power/5` — a 1:1 algebraic restatement of
the target.

Adding `opponent_inferred_gun_heat` to `FIRE_POWER_LEAKAGE_COLS` and
re-running revealed a **second-order leak one step further out**:

```
Fire Power Prediction (11,059 test samples) — leaky run #2
  Random Forest: MAE=0.001, R²=1.000
  Top feature:  escape_angle_coverage (1.000)
```

`escape_angle_coverage` is the integral of `lat_vel / MEA` over the wave's
flight, normalized to [0, 1]. At the fire-detection tick MEA is recomputed
from the new bullet's speed, so any wave-derived column refreshed on the
fire tick (the entire `WAVE_DERIVED_COLS` family — `escape_angle_coverage`,
`opponent_wave_*`, `ticks_since_opponent_fired`, `gf_current_at_power_*`)
encodes `bullet_speed = 20 − 3·power`.

Once **all twelve** columns are excluded (4 algebraic identities + 8 wave-
refresh columns), the model is finally honest:

```
Fire Power Prediction (11,059 test samples) — honest run
  Mean baseline: MAE=0.712
  Random Forest: MAE=0.206, R²=0.809  (71 % MAE reduction)

Top 10 features:
  mea_for_our_bullet:                    0.32
  our_bullet_speed:                      0.26
  opponent_energy:                       0.16
  energy_ratio:                          0.06
  scan_arc_width:                        0.03
  opponent_time_since_direction_change:  0.02
  opponent_time_since_velocity_change:   0.02
  distance:                              0.02
```

This is a real result. The dominant predictor is **our own gun cycle**
(`our_bullet_speed` and `mea_for_our_bullet`) — the rhythm of our fires
correlates with the rhythm of theirs because both bots wait out gun heat
in similar windows. After that, opponent energy and the energy ratio
gate how much they're willing to spend per shot. Distance, scan-arc
geometry, and opponent reaction-time signals each contribute a few
percent. R² = 0.81 says you can pin down the next opponent power to
roughly ±0.2 (out of a [0.1, 3.0] range) given a state snapshot; that's
useful for energy-economy modelling but far from solved.

The current `FIRE_POWER_LEAKAGE_COLS` in
[`_loader.py`](../../intuition/_loader.py) is the canonical exclusion
list for this target.

### 6. KS-adaptation ranking is sensitive to the choice of baseline window

Two notebooks now compute "is this opponent adapting" via KS test on
`opponent_lateral_velocity`, with surprisingly different rankings:

| Bot | nb05 (rounds 0–4 vs 30–34) | nb09 (round 0 vs mean of 30–34) |
|---|---:|---:|
| Shadow 3.83c | 0.065 | **0.325** |
| Roborio 1.2.4 | 0.063 | 0.187 |
| DrussGT 3.1.7 | **0.168** | 0.101 |
| Engineer 0.5.4 | (not top) | 0.112 |
| Domogled 1.2 | 0.074 | (not top) |

Shadow leaps from 13th to 1st when the baseline is round 0 alone instead
of rounds 0–4. The interpretation is that **Shadow's adaptation accumulates
gradually**: even round 4 already differs from round 0, so pooling rounds
0–4 dilutes the early baseline. DrussGT, conversely, has a **fast initial
shift** that's already settled by round 4 — pooling 0–4 captures it,
single-round-0 doesn't.

This means there's no single "is the bot learning" number. Three different
metrics emerge:

1. **Late-vs-early shift** (nb05 style) — captures bots whose final movement
   is far from initial movement, regardless of trajectory.
2. **Cumulative drift from round 0** (nb09 style) — captures bots whose
   movement keeps drifting throughout the match.
3. **Round-by-round novelty** (cumulative-KS labelling, nb09 cell 9) — fires
   when the running distribution becomes statistically distinct from round 0.

For an in-game "is the opponent currently adapting" detector, option 3 is
the right shape: it produces a per-round binary label that can be evaluated
online. The persisted `intuition/adaptation_labels.csv` (1,700 rows,
6.5 % positive rate) is the seed for any future supervised "detect
adaptation" model.

### 7. Wave-fingerprint classifier accuracy is much lower than PCA spread suggested — but trajectory features close some of the gap

Phase 3 reported "PC1+PC2 explain 66 % of inter-bot wave-stat variance" and
took that as evidence wave fingerprints would identify bots quickly. The
explicit RandomForest in notebook 10 says otherwise. The original 6-stat
aggregate (`mean_power`, `std_power`, `mean_dist`, `std_dist`,
`mean_abs_lat`, `corr_pow_dist`) topped out at ~28 % CV top-1 across 44
families. Adding four trajectory features — `power_trend` (slope of fire
power across the window), `dist_trend`, `power_autocorr` (lag-1
autocorrelation of power), `power_range` (max − min) — moves the result up
by ~6 percentage points:

| N (fires) | 6-stat CV (Phase 5 v1) | 10-stat CV (Phase 5 v2) | Random |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 5   | 0.288 ± 0.036 | **0.285 ± 0.034** | 0.023 |
| 10  | 0.283 ± 0.057 | **0.285 ± 0.050** | 0.023 |
| 20  | 0.285 ± 0.038 | **0.349 ± 0.034** | 0.023 |
| 50  | 0.147 ± 0.015 | **0.208 ± 0.014** | 0.023 |
| 100 | 0.187 ± 0.048 | **0.251 ± 0.042** | 0.023 |
| 200 | 0.195 ± 0.026 | **0.302 ± 0.027** | 0.023 |

The trajectory features carry ≈ 39 % of total RF importance — `power_range`
(0.108), `power_trend` (0.103), `power_autocorr` (0.092), `dist_trend`
(0.086) — comparable to or larger than the static features they joined
(each 0.09–0.12). The biggest jump is at N=20: trajectory drift only
becomes visible once the window contains enough fires to fit a slope.

The PCA result still holds — it just measures something different
(variance explained) than what the classifier needs (linear separability
across all class boundaries simultaneously). 35 % top-1 over 44 classes
with ten cheap statistics per perspective is the honest ceiling of this
fingerprint family; further gains would need raw-sequence models, not more
hand-crafted aggregates.

### 8. Two more leakage audits — `opponent_fired` collapses, adaptation classifier collapses

Recommendations 5 and 6 were carried out in this same phase rather than
deferred:

- **`opponent_fired` (notebook 04, original 98.88 % claim).** With
  `WAVE_DERIVED_COLS` excluded *including* `opponent_inferred_gun_heat`
  (which jumps to `1 + power/5` on the fire-detection tick), all four
  classifiers collapse onto the always-predict-zero majority baseline
  (test accuracy 0.931, macro F1 0.49). The Phase 1 result was the
  gun-heat counter restating the target. Per-tick state-of-world alone
  does not predict fires — a real model would need a sliding window of
  opponent state, not a single tick. RF feature importance is now
  uniform (top feature `energy_ratio` at 0.097, distributed across the
  remaining 48 columns).
- **Adaptation classifier (notebook 09, recommendation 5).** Per-(opponent,
  round) RF with `GroupKFold(5)` on `opponent_bot` predicting
  `is_adapting_now` from per-round aggregates of velocity / lat-vel /
  distance / heading-delta / fire count: **accuracy 0.914 ± 0.080 vs
  majority baseline 0.935** (worse than majority). ROC AUC degenerates
  to NaN because only 10 of 50 bots ever trigger the detector and
  several CV folds contain zero positive samples. Adaptation is a
  between-round drift property; within-round means and stds wash it
  out. The right shape is a sequence model on the round-by-round
  distribution stream — but the KS-distance-from-baseline IS that
  shape, and it doesn't need supervision because it produces the label
  directly.

Both results are **negative findings worth keeping**: they bound what the
current per-tick / per-round feature schemas can do, and they replace two
old "100 % accuracy" headlines with honest ceilings.

## Schema / loader observations

- The new `_loader.py` helpers (`numeric_feature_cols`, `attach_opponent_bot`,
  `WAVE_DERIVED_COLS`, `FIRE_POWER_LEAKAGE_COLS`, `drop_redundant_features`)
  paid off across all four converted notebooks. The string-column-into-RF
  failure mode that broke notebook 05 mid-Phase 4 surfaced precisely once
  in Phase 5 (cell 17 of nb05's first run, where the persistence baseline
  needed the target column kept in the matrix) and was fixed by reading the
  feature matrix once with an explicit `.astype(np.float32)` cast at
  construction time. That cast is the right place to fail loudly: it raises
  with a clear column name instead of bubbling up from inside sklearn.
- nb09 and nb10 both observed an `overflow encountered in cast` warning
  during the float64→float32 downcast in `_loader.load_stratified`. Doesn't
  affect the analysis (only an `inf` round-trip in unused columns), but
  worth tracking — the loader should clip before downcasting.
- nb10's `pd.concat` warning ("future deprecation of empty-frame inclusion")
  is benign now but should get a `dtype=` hint or pre-filter to silence it
  going forward.
